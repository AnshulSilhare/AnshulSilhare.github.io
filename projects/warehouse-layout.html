Warehouse Layout Optimization: Flow Engineering

DomainWarehousing & Distribution
FocusZoning & Cross-Docking Flow
MethodologyLean Principles, SKU Velocity profiling
Operational ToolFacility Design Layouts

Situation

Traditional warehouse operations suffer from spatial inefficiencies over time as product ranges expand. In the baseline facility, material handlers experienced congested, overlapping paths where inbound shipments conflicted with outbound staging. This chaotic "spaghetti flow" led to picking delays, higher worker fatigue, and safety risks from forklifts crossing packing zones.

Task

My objective was to redesign the warehouse floor layout to resolve bottlenecks, segregate inbound and outbound material flows, and optimize product slotting to maximize daily picking throughput.

Action

I formulated and executed a zoning and slotting optimization model:

  • Flow Audit: Traced material handling routes and created a "spaghetti diagram" to map the exact paths of workers and equipment, highlighting high-collision hotspots.
  • Zoning Segregation: Restructured the facility design by separating Inbound (Receiving) operations from Outbound (Shipping) staging. Dedicated distinct docks to each phase, preventing process overlap and cross-traffic delays.
  • SKU Velocity slotting: Categorized SKU turnover rates. Placed Fast-moving (Class A) SKUs immediately adjacent to the main packaging lines, reducing average picker travel distances.
  • Safety & Traffic Separation: Mapped designated forklift transit lanes, separating them completely from pedestrian packing benches to lower warehouse floor safety hazards.

Result

Operational Benefits
Optimization Area Baseline Performance Optimized Layout Operational Impact
Material Flow Intersecting (Spaghetti) Dedicated In/Out Zones Eliminated inbound/outbound dock congestion.
Picker Travel Time High (unstructured storage) Velocity-based slotting Reduces travel distance by keeping Class A items near packaging.

Reflection

This project illustrates that warehouse productivity is highly dependent on spatial discipline. Simply increasing warehouse staff without organizing the layout will worsen congestion and lower picking speeds. Applying basic zoning and SKU velocity profiling is a low-cost, high-return strategy to unlock hidden warehouse capacity.
