Situation

Students entering the field of Data Analytics are flooded with conflicting advice. Online bootcamps advocate learning complex Python/ML libraries, academic advisors recommend formal degrees, and online forums emphasize visualization tools. To build a robust career entry strategy, I wanted to replace subjective opinions with objective, raw job market data.

Task

My objective was to analyze a dataset of over 100,000 job listings in the United States, cleaning and processing nested arrays of skills to identify the most in-demand capabilities, trace salary outliers, map degree requirements, and determine the optimal skill combinations for aspiring Analysts.

Action

I utilized a clean Python analytics stack to ingest, clean, and visualize the dataset:

  • Data Cleansing & Explode: Filtered the Luke Barousse Hugging Face dataset (785K+ listings) down to US Data Analyst listings. Cleaned nested skill arrays by using Pandas' pandas.explode() method to separate individual skills into distinct analysis rows.
  • Comparative Skill Mapping: Aggregated skill count metrics grouped by roles. Checked skill distribution rates for Data Analysts vs. Data Engineers and Data Scientists.
  • Degree & Work-Setting Audit: Processed raw text fields in the listings to check for explicit degree requirements and remote work availability metrics.
  • Strategic Priority Model: Mapped demand percentages against median salaries to identify high-value skill clusters. Developed a structured study priority framework based on the findings.

Reflection

This analysis proves that the internet overemphasizes flashy skills like Python/ML for entry-level analyst roles, whereas the market overwhelmingly prioritizes SQL, Excel, and visualization foundations. Letting data guide my learning path saved weeks of misallocated study time and helped me target the exact skills that recruiters actively look for.
